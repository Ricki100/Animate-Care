 STATEMENT OF PURPOSE 
 
 1 Introduction 
 
 This Statement of Purpose is produced in accordance with the Children’s Homes (England) Regulations 2015 and associated Quality Standards. It describes the aims, ethos, and operational arrangements of Mardle Children’s Home, delivered by Animate Social Care. 
 
 The welfare of children is at the centre of all practice. The home is committed to providing safe, stable, and nurturing care that promotes emotional security, resilience, positive identity, and long-term improved outcomes for children and young people. 
 
 This Statement of Purpose reflects a trauma-informed and relationship-based model of care. Practice is underpinned by the understanding that many children who come into residential care have experienced disrupted attachment, adversity, exploitation, loss, and inconsistent caregiving. 
 
 2 Purpose of the Home 
 
 Mardle Children’s Home provides care and accommodation for up to two children and young people aged 8–17 years. 
 
 The home supports children who present with a range of social, emotional, and mental health needs (SEMH), including those linked to trauma, attachment difficulties, emotional dysregulation, and experiences of contextual risk including exploitation. 
 
 The home is designed to provide consistent, structured, and therapeutic care within a relationship-based environment where children can experience safety, stability, and predictable boundaries. 
 
 Children are supported regardless of their race, religion, culture, disability, gender identity, sexual orientation, or neurodiversity. 
 3 Ethos and Philosophy of Care 
 
 The ethos of the home is grounded in the belief that children and young people are best supported through consistent relationships, clear boundaries, and a deep understanding of developmental trauma and adolescent behaviour. 
 
 Practice is built on the principle that behaviour communicates need, and that children’s responses must be understood within the context of their developmental stage, lived experience, and relational history. 
 
 The home operates a relational and trauma-informed approach which recognises that: 
 
 Trust is developed through consistency rather than instruction. 
 Boundaries are a form of care, not punishment. 
 Relationships are central to change. 
 Children may reject care while simultaneously needing it. 
 Repair following rupture is a key part of emotional development. 
 
 Staff are expected to provide care that is both emotionally available and firmly boundaried. Children are supported through predictable responses, professional consistency, and reflective practice. 
 
 Core Practice Identity 
 
 The home’s practice is underpinned by a belief that adolescents require both connection and containment to develop safely. 
 
 Children are supported by staff who understand that: 
 Adolescence is a developmental stage characterised by heightened emotional intensity, risk-taking, and identity formation. 
 Behaviour must be interpreted developmentally, not solely punitively. 
 Safety and relationship can exist simultaneously, even during periods of conflict. 
 Professional consistency creates emotional security over time. 
 
 The home is committed to providing care that is relational, thoughtful, and informed by an understanding of trauma, attachment, and adolescent development. 
 
 4. Therapeutic Model of Care 
 4.1 Relationship-Based and Trauma-Informed Practice 
 
 Animate Social Care recognises that many children who come into residential care have experienced adversity, disrupted attachments, trauma, loss, neglect, exploitation, and significant relational harm. These experiences often affect a child's ability to trust adults, regulate emotions, form healthy relationships, and maintain a positive sense of identity. 
 
 Our approach is rooted in relationship-based practice. We recognise that meaningful and lasting change occurs through safe, consistent, and trusting relationships. The staff team are committed to providing children with experiences that promote emotional security, resilience, and a renewed sense of belonging. 
 
 We work in partnership with children, their families where appropriate, social workers, education providers, health professionals, and other agencies to ensure that care is holistic, coordinated, and responsive to individual need. 
 
 The home operates a trauma-informed model of care which seeks to understand the reasons behind behaviour rather than focusing solely on the behaviour itself. Staff are encouraged to remain curious, reflective, and compassionate when supporting children through periods of distress, challenge, or uncertainty. 
 
 Children are supported to develop insight into their thoughts, feelings, and behaviours, helping them make sense of their experiences and develop healthier ways of managing adversity. 
 
 4.2 PACE and Dyadic Developmental Practice (DDP) 
 
 The home's therapeutic approach is underpinned by the PACE model and principles of Dyadic Developmental Practice (DDP). 
 
 PACE promotes: 
 
 Playfulness 
 Acceptance 
 Curiosity 
 Empathy 
 
 These principles guide everyday interactions between staff and children and support the development of secure attachments, emotional regulation, and healthy relationships. 
 
 Children who have experienced trauma frequently communicate distress through behaviour rather than words. PACE enables staff to remain connected to the child whilst maintaining appropriate boundaries and expectations. 
 
 The model encourages staff to: 
 
 Remain emotionally available during periods of distress. 
 Understand behaviour as communication. 
 Promote emotional safety. 
 Support children to explore feelings without fear of judgement. 
 Build trust through consistency and authenticity. 
 
 The principles of PACE are embedded throughout daily practice, care planning, key work, behaviour support, safeguarding interventions, and multi-agency collaboration. 
 
 4.3 Working With Children Rather Than Doing to Them 
 
 4.4 Collaborative Problem Solving and Co-Production 
 
 Animate Social Care believes children and young people should be active participants in decisions affecting their lives. 
 
 Our philosophy is one of working with children rather than doing things to them. 
 
 The home operates an open-door culture where children are encouraged to approach staff, discuss concerns, challenge decisions respectfully and contribute meaningfully to plans which affect their lives. 
 
 We recognise that children are more likely to engage positively with plans they understand, influence, and feel ownership of. Wherever possible, children are therefore involved in developing the plans and strategies designed to support them. 
 
 This may include: 
 
 Care planning. 
 Risk assessments and risk management. 
 Safety planning. 
 Independence planning. 
 House meetings. 
 Activity planning. 
 Reviews and professional meetings where appropriate. 
 
 Staff are encouraged to engage children in open, honest, and transparent conversations about concerns, risks, expectations, and decision-making. Children are invited to share their views, identify what is important to them and contribute to finding solutions. 
 
 Staff are equally open about professional responsibilities, safeguarding duties, and situations where certain boundaries cannot be compromised. The focus of discussion is not whether children are kept safe, but how adults and children can work together to achieve safety, stability and positive outcomes in ways that are meaningful and achievable for the child. 
 
 This approach reduces unnecessary power struggles, promotes mutual respect, and helps children understand the reasons behind professional decision-making. 
 
 Experience has consistently shown that children are more likely to engage with plans they have helped to create than those which are simply imposed upon them. Co-production therefore forms a central part of care planning, safety planning, independence work, and daily practice within the home. 
 
 Where restrictions or safeguarding measures are required, staff will explain the reasons for these decisions and seek to involve children wherever possible. We believe that understanding promotes cooperation, preserves dignity, and strengthens relationships. 
 
 The home believes that effective safeguarding and positive relationships are not opposing concepts. Children can be given a meaningful voice whilst adults retain responsibility for keeping them safe. 
 
 Children should know where they stand with the adults caring for them and understand both the support available to them and the boundaries that exist to keep them safe. 
 
 This approach is adapted according to each child's age, understanding, communication style and developmental stage, ensuring that all children can participate meaningfully in decisions affecting their lives. 
 
 4.5 Understanding the Teenage Brain 
 
 The home places significant importance on understanding child and adolescent development, recognising that children's emotional, social, and cognitive development may differ from their chronological age due to trauma, attachment disruption, neurodiversity, exploitation, or adverse childhood experiences. 
 Expectations, consequences, rewards, and interventions are tailored to the child's age, understanding, and developmental presentation. 
 
 The teenage years represent a period of rapid neurological, emotional, and social change. During this stage of development, children often demonstrate: 
 
 Increased impulsivity. 
 Heightened emotional responses. 
 Greater sensitivity to peer influence. 
 Increased risk-taking behaviour. 
 Developing consequential thinking. 
 Identity exploration. 
 A growing desire for independence and autonomy. 
 Understanding these developmental processes shapes the way staff respond to behaviour. 
 
 We recognise that adolescents can appear capable and mature in some situations whilst requiring significant guidance and support in others. Behaviour which may appear irrational, challenging, or oppositional is often linked to normal developmental processes, compounded by previous trauma or adverse experiences. 
 
 This understanding encourages staff to remain curious rather than punitive, reflective rather than reactive, and supportive whilst maintaining clear boundaries. 
 
 Containment and boundary setting remain essential components of adolescent development. Although boundaries may be challenged or resisted, they provide predictability, consistency, and emotional safety. 
 
 Children need to know where they stand with the adults caring for them. Consistent responses, clear expectations, and reliable relationships help young people develop trust, emotional security, and confidence in those supporting them. 
 Whilst the home has considerable expertise in supporting adolescents, practice is adapted to meet each child's individual developmental stage and presenting needs. 
 
 Our role is not simply to manage behaviour but to help children understand themselves, develop emotional regulation and make sense of the world around them. 
 
 4.6 Restorative and Reflective Practice 
 
 Animate adopts a restorative approach to behaviour support and relationship building. 
 We believe children are more than the mistakes they make. 
 Whilst concerning patterns of behaviour are identified, assessed, and managed appropriately, children are supported to understand that opportunities for repair, learning and growth are always available. 
 
 The focus is on: 
 
 Reflection. 
 Accountability. 
 Learning. 
 Repairing relationships. 
 Restoring trust. 
 
 Consequences, where necessary, will be proportionate, meaningful, and focused on helping children understand the impact of their actions rather than creating shame. 
 Children are supported to make amends in ways that are achievable, constructive, and developmentally appropriate. 
 
 Following incidents, restorative conversations provide opportunities for children and staff to reflect together, understand what happened and consider how similar situations may be managed differently in the future. 
 
 The home promotes a culture where mistakes become opportunities for learning and where positive relationships can survive difficult moments. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 5. Safeguarding and Protection of Children 
 Safeguarding Philosophy 
 
 The welfare and safety of children is paramount in all aspects of service delivery. 
 
 Animate Social Care recognises that safeguarding extends beyond protecting children from immediate harm. Effective safeguarding requires adults to understand the wider contexts in which children live, develop relationships and experience risk. 
 
 We recognise that children who have experienced trauma, adversity, disrupted attachments, exploitation, or neglect may not always recognise danger, may minimise concerns or may actively resist intervention from adults seeking to protect them. 
 
 Our role is to remain professionally curious, persistent, and child-centred whilst maintaining a clear focus on safety and welfare. 
 
 Children are supported to understand that safeguarding measures are not punitive but are implemented because they are valued, important and deserving of protection. 
 
 Whilst safeguarding interventions may at times feel intrusive or restrictive to young people, staff remain committed to explaining decisions, maintaining relationships, and repairing any disruption to trust that may occur as a consequence of keeping children safe. 
 
 The home seeks to engage children in understanding and contributing to plans designed to keep them safe. Whilst safeguarding responsibilities remain with adults, children are encouraged to participate in identifying strategies that are realistic, meaningful, and achievable. This approach supports greater engagement, promotes shared ownership of safety planning, and helps children develop a better understanding of risk, responsibility, and personal safety. 
 
 Child Protection 
 
 The home is committed to protecting children from abuse, neglect, exploitation, and significant harm. 
 All safeguarding concerns are responded to promptly and in accordance with: 
 
 Norfolk Safeguarding Children Partnership procedures. 
 Children's Homes Regulations 2015. 
 Working Together to Safeguard Children. 
 Keeping Children Safe in Education (where applicable). 
 The home's safeguarding and child protection policies. 
 
 Staff understand their individual responsibility to identify, respond to and report concerns immediately. 
 Children are supported to understand their rights, access advocacy services, and speak openly about any concerns affecting their safety and wellbeing. 
 
 Contextual Safeguarding 
 
 Animate recognises that risks to children frequently occur beyond the home environment. 
 Our safeguarding practice is informed by contextual safeguarding principles and acknowledges that harm may occur within: 
 
 Peer groups. 
 Schools and educational settings. 
 Community spaces. 
 Public transport. 
 Social media platforms. 
 Online gaming environments. 
 Local neighbourhoods. 
 
 The staff team work collaboratively with children, families, social workers, education providers, police, and specialist services to understand and reduce risks within these wider contexts. 
 
 Safeguarding assessments therefore consider not only the child but also the environments in which they spend their time. 
 
 Child Sexual Exploitation, Child Criminal Exploitation and Serious Youth Violence 
 
 The Registered Manager has extensive experience supporting children affected by exploitation, county lines activity, criminal exploitation, serious youth violence, and grooming. 
 
 The home recognises that exploited children are victims of abuse regardless of how their behaviour may present. 
 
 Children may: 
 Appear to make unsafe choices. 
 Be reluctant to engage with professionals. 
 Display loyalty towards exploiters. 
 Minimise risk. 
 Go missing from care. 
 Become involved in criminal activity. 
 
 Staff are trained to recognise indicators of exploitation and understand the complex dynamics that influence children's decision-making. 
 
 Safeguarding responses focus upon: 
 
 Building trusted relationships. 
 Understanding the child's lived experience. 
 Disrupting exploitation. 
 Increasing protective factors. 
 Multi-agency collaboration. 
 Supporting children to recognise and understand risk. 
 The home promotes a culture of professional curiosity and recognises that behavioural changes may indicate underlying safeguarding concerns. 
 
 Missing From Care 
 
 The home recognises that children who go missing are at increased risk of harm, exploitation, victimisation, and serious youth violence. Robust procedures are in place to respond promptly and proportionately to any missing-from-care episode. 
 
 Individual risk assessments and safety plans identify known vulnerabilities, triggers, locations of concern, peer associations, and contextual safeguarding risks. 
 
 Animate Social Care works in partnership with Norfolk Police and relevant agencies and uses the Philomena Protocol where appropriate. The Philomena Protocol enables key information regarding a child's vulnerabilities, presentation, locations, and known associates to be readily available, supporting a swift and informed response when a child is reported missing. 
 
 Staff understand that missing episodes are often symptomatic of underlying unmet needs, exploitation, trauma, or emotional distress. Therefore, responses focus not only on locating the child safely but also on understanding the reasons behind the episode and strengthening future safety planning. 
 The Registered Manager, placing authority, parents (where appropriate), police, and relevant professionals are notified in line with statutory requirements. 
 
 
 
 
 
 The staff team work proactively to reduce missing episodes through: 
 
 Relationship building. 
 Safety planning. 
 Restorative conversations. 
 Contextual safeguarding approaches. 
 Understanding the function of the behaviour. 
 
 Following any missing episode, children are offered the opportunity to participate in a return home interview and staff review existing plans to identify any additional safeguards required. 
 
 Risk Assessment and Safety Planning 
 
 Risk assessments are viewed as dynamic documents which support safe and informed decision-making. 
 
 Risk assessments are developed collaboratively wherever possible and take account of: 
 
 The child's views. 
 Historical information. 
 Current presenting concerns. 
 Strengths and protective factors. 
 Contextual risks. 
 Emerging information. 
 
 Children are encouraged to contribute to their own safety planning. 
 
 We recognise that children are more likely to engage with plans they have helped to create and understand. 
 
 Plans are reviewed regularly and updated following significant events, changes in circumstances or emerging concerns. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 Online Safety 
 
 Animate recognises that the online world forms a significant part of children's social, emotional, and developmental lives. 
 
 The home is committed to supporting children to access technology safely whilst recognising the risks associated with: 
 
 Online grooming. 
 Child sexual exploitation. 
 Criminal exploitation. 
 Radicalisation. 
 Sextortion. 
 Cyberbullying. 
 Harmful online content. 
 
 The home provides Wi-Fi access with appropriate parental controls and monitoring systems. 
 
 Televisions, gaming devices, and internet-enabled equipment are subject to age-appropriate safety measures. 
 Staff understand that online activity can present significant safeguarding concerns and are trained to identify indicators of risk. 
 
 The home promotes open conversations about online activity and encourages children to seek support when they encounter situations that make them feel uncomfortable or unsafe. 
 
 Education around online exploitation forms part of the home's wider safeguarding programme. 
 
 Children are supported to develop the knowledge, confidence and critical thinking skills required to navigate the online world safely. 
 
 Home Security and Surveillance 
 
 The home operates Ring Doorbell technology as an additional safeguarding measure. 
 
 Children, families and placing authorities are informed of its purpose and operation. 
 
 
 
 
 The system is used proportionately and only for legitimate safeguarding purposes, including: 
 Monitoring visitors to the home. 
 Supporting investigations relating to safeguarding concerns. 
 Assisting when children are reported missing. 
 
 The home does not operate internal surveillance systems. 
 
 Children's privacy, dignity, and rights are always respected. 
 
 The use, storage and sharing of footage is governed by the home's data protection and surveillance policies and complies with relevant GDPR requirements. 
 
 Emergency and Fire Safety 
 
 The home maintains robust emergency planning arrangements to ensure the safety and welfare of all children, staff, and visitors. 
 
 These arrangements include: 
 
 Fire safety training. 
 Routine fire drills. 
 Weekly testing of fire systems. 
 Fire detection and alarm systems. 
 Fire doors and firefighting equipment. 
 Emergency evacuation procedures. 
 Hazard reporting and maintenance processes. 
 
 Children receive information about emergency procedures upon admission and are supported to understand how to keep themselves and others safe. 
 
 6. Children's Participation, Wishes and Feelings 
 Promoting Children's Voice 
 
 Animate Social Care is committed to ensuring that children and young people are listened to, respected and actively involved in decisions affecting their lives. 
 
 Children have a right to express their views, wishes and feelings and to know that those views will be taken seriously. 
 
 The home promotes a culture where children are encouraged to share their opinions openly and safely, regardless of age, ability, communication style, culture, disability, gender identity, faith, or background. 
 
 Children receive information in formats appropriate to their age, understanding, and developmental stage. The home provides separate Children's Guides for younger children and older young people, recognising that children engage differently depending upon age, developmental presentation, and communication preference. 
 Communication methods and participation opportunities are adapted to ensure all children can understand information and contribute meaningfully to decisions affecting their lives. 
 
 Children are supported to contribute to: 
 
 Care planning. 
 Risk assessments. 
 Safety planning. 
 Placement planning meetings. 
 Reviews. 
 Independence planning. 
 Activity planning. 
 Menu planning. 
 House meetings. 
 Development of the home environment. 
 
 The staff team actively seek children's views and ensure that participation is embedded within daily practice rather than restricted to formal meetings. 
 
 Building Trust and Communication 
 
 Animate Social Care recognises that meaningful participation is dependent upon trusted relationships. 
 
 Children are encouraged to express both positive and negative feelings and are supported to do so in a manner that is safe and respectful. 
 
 Staff create opportunities for informal conversations, key work sessions and reflective discussions that allow children to explore worries, concerns, and aspirations. 
 
 Children are encouraged to challenge decisions respectfully and ask questions about matters affecting them. 
 
 Where decisions are made that differ from a child's wishes, staff will explain the reasons for those decisions and ensure the child understands how their views were considered. 
 
 This approach promotes transparency, fairness, and mutual respect. 
 
 Key Work and Individual Support 
 
 Each child will have a named Key Worker who takes responsibility for coordinating aspects of their day-to-day care and supporting the implementation of their care plan. 
 
 Key work provides dedicated time for children and staff to explore: 
 
 Emotional wellbeing. 
 Relationships. 
 Education. 
 Identity. 
 Safety. 
 Health. 
 Independence skills. 
 Future aspirations. 
 
 The key worker role supports the development of trusted relationships and provides children with a consistent adult who understands their individual journey. 
 
 Whilst each child will have a designated Key Worker, all members of the staff team share responsibility for supporting children and promoting positive outcomes. 
 
 House Meetings 
 
 Regular house meetings provide children with opportunities to influence the operation and development of the home. 
 
 Children are encouraged to contribute ideas, raise concerns, and celebrate achievements. 
 
 Topics may include: 
 Activities and holidays. 
 Menus and meal planning. 
 House rules and routines. 
 Decoration and personalisation of shared spaces. 
 Community participation. 
 Suggestions for improving the service. 
 
 Feedback provided by children is recorded and considered by the management team. 
 
 Where suggestions cannot be implemented, children are provided with an explanation and alternative solutions explored where appropriate. 
 
 Advocacy 
 
 Children have the right to independent advocacy and support. 
 
 Information regarding advocacy services is provided in formats appropriate to the child's age and understanding. 
 Staff actively encourage children to access advocacy support where they require assistance expressing their views, raising concerns, or participating in meetings. 
 
 Children are supported to contact advocates independently if they wish to do so. 
 
 Advocates are welcomed into the home and supported to undertake their role free from influence or interference. 
 Equality, Diversity, and Inclusion 
 
 Animate Social Care values and celebrates diversity. 
 
 Children are supported to develop a positive understanding of their identity and to feel respected, accepted and valued for who they are. 
 
 The home is committed to promoting equality of opportunity and ensuring that children are not disadvantaged because of: 
 
 Race. 
 Ethnicity. 
 Religion or belief. 
 Disability. 
 Neurodiversity. 
 Gender identity. 
 Sexual orientation. 
 Cultural background. 
 
 Care planning considers each child's individual identity, culture, beliefs, and experiences. 
 
 Children are supported to access community resources, cultural opportunities, and faith-based services where these form an important part of their identity. 
 
 Complaints and Concerns 
 
 Animate Social Care welcomes feedback and recognises the importance of children being able to raise concerns without fear of negative consequences. 
 
 Children are encouraged to discuss worries informally with staff wherever possible. However, they also have access to a formal complaint’s procedure. 
 
 Information about how to make a complaint is provided upon admission and is available in child-friendly formats throughout the home. 
 
 Children may seek support from: 
 
 Their Key Worker. 
 The Registered Manager. 
 Their Social Worker. 
 Independent advocates. 
 Ofsted. 
 Other relevant professionals. 
 
 All complaints are taken seriously and investigated promptly, fairly, and transparently. 
 
 Children are informed of the outcome of complaints and any actions arising from investigations. 
 
 Complaints are monitored by the Registered Manager as part of the home's quality assurance and service improvement processes. 
 
 
 
 
 7. Education, Health, Emotional Wellbeing, and Independence 
 Promoting Positive Outcomes 
 
 Animate Social Care is committed to supporting children and young people to achieve positive and sustainable outcomes across all areas of their lives. 
 
 We recognise that progress looks different for every child and that achievement should be measured against individual starting points, experiences, and personal goals. 
 
 Many children arriving at the home may have experienced disrupted education, trauma, instability, relationship breakdowns, exploitation, or adverse childhood experiences. These experiences can impact confidence, self-esteem, emotional wellbeing, and aspirations for the future. 
 
 The role of the home is to provide consistent support, encouragement and opportunities that enable children to recognise their strengths, develop resilience, and achieve their potential. 
 Progress is celebrated in all forms, whether academic, emotional, social, behavioural, or practical. 
 
 Education and Learning 
 
 The home recognises education as a protective factor and an important contributor to future opportunities, independence, and wellbeing. 
 
 Children are supported to access and engage with education, training, and employment opportunities appropriate to their individual needs, abilities, and aspirations. 
 
 Staff work closely with: 
 
 Schools. 
 Alternative education providers. 
 Virtual School Heads. 
 SEN teams. 
 Tutors. 
 Further education providers. 
 Children are encouraged to develop positive attitudes towards learning and are supported to overcome barriers that may have previously impacted educational engagement. 
 
 
 
 
 
 The home promotes: 
 
 School attendance. 
 Educational achievement. 
 Homework and revision support. 
 Access to educational resources. 
 Participation in enrichment opportunities. 
 Staff celebrate effort and progress and help children recognise their achievements. 
 
 Emotional Health and Wellbeing

 9. Admissions, Matching and Care Planning 
 Admissions Philosophy 
 
 Animate Social Care is committed to ensuring that admissions are planned, thoughtful and centred upon the needs, safety, and welfare of children. 
 
 The home recognises that the quality of matching decisions has a significant impact upon placement stability, children's experiences, and outcomes. 
 
 Animate Social Care has expertise in supporting adolescents; however, care is tailored to meet the developmental needs of each child and support is adapted according to age, understanding and presentation. 
 
 Admissions are therefore considered carefully to ensure that the home can meet a child's identified needs whilst maintaining a safe, nurturing, and positive environment for all children living in the home. 
 
 The home seeks to balance opportunities for children requiring care with the responsibility to protect existing relationships, routines, and placement stability. 
 
 Children the Home is Registered to Support 
 
 Mardle Children's Home provides care and accommodation for up to two children and young people aged 8–17 years. 
 
 The home is designed to support children presenting with:

 Experiences of neglect or abuse. 
 Contextual safeguarding concerns. 
 Child Sexual Exploitation (CSE). 
 Child Criminal Exploitation (CCE).

 Challenges associated with adolescence and identity development.

 Prior to admission, the Registered Manager will gather and review all available information relating to the child. 
 
 
 
 This may include: 
 
 Referral documentation.

 Education information. 
 Health information. 
 Risk assessments. 
 Previous placement information.

 The child's strengths and needs. 
 Risks and vulnerabilities. 
 Protective factors. 
 Compatibility with existing children. 
 Resources required to support the placement. 
 The home's ability to meet identified needs. 
 
 Where information is limited, the Registered Manager will seek clarification from the placing authority before a decision is made. 
 
 Matching Considerations 
 
 Matching decisions are central to the home's commitment to providing safe and stable care. 
 A comprehensive impact and compatibility assessment is undertaken before any admission. 
 
 
 The assessment considers: 
 
 The needs of the referred child. 
 The needs of children already living in the home. 
 Known risks and vulnerabilities. 
 Developmental stages. 
 Peer dynamics. 
 Exploitation concerns. 
 Missing from care risks. 
 Criminal associations. 
 Emotional wellbeing. 
 Bedroom arrangements. 
 Gender considerations. 
 Cultural and identity needs. 
 The welfare of all children remains paramount throughout the matching process.

 The home recognises that there may be occasions where emergency placements are required.

 14. Conclusion 
 
 Animate Social Care is committed to providing high-quality, relationship-based care that promotes safety, stability, belonging and positive outcomes for children and young people. 
 
 The home recognises that children entering residential care may have experienced trauma, loss, adversity, disrupted relationships, and significant safeguarding challenges. Our role is to provide a safe, nurturing and developmentally responsive environment where children are supported to understand themselves, build resilience and develop confidence in their future. 
 
 Practice is underpinned by strong relationships, clear boundaries, co-production, contextual safeguarding, and a commitment to understanding behaviour within the context of children's experiences and developmental needs. 
 Children are encouraged to have a meaningful voice in decisions affecting their lives and are supported to participate in ways that are appropriate to their age, understanding, and developmental stage. 
 
 The home believes that effective care is built upon honesty, consistency, and trust. Children should know where they stand with the adults caring for them and should experience relationships that remain dependable during both positive and challenging times. 
 
 Animate Social Care is committed to working alongside children, families, and partner agencies to help children achieve safety, stability, independence, and a sense of belonging. 
 
 The aim is that every child leaves the home with increased confidence, stronger relationships, improved life opportunities, and the knowledge that they have been cared for by adults who genuinely believed in their potential.
